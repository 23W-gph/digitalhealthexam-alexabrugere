Smoking in the USA


Cigarette Use and Access to Information about Smoking

Research indicates there is a correlation between age and current, occasional, or past smoking habits. In a study from 2020, it was found that older individuals tend to have greater access to information regarding the adverse effects of smoking, yet tend to smoke more. Consequently, younger individuals exhibit lower smoking rates, despite less access to information about the risks associated with smoking. For example, respondents aged 15-20 lacked awareness about the dangers of smoking, despite the fact that most of them had not attempted smoking.

Youth and Smoking Data

According to data from 2012 and 2013, 1 in 16 adolescents (6.1 percent) in the USA had smoked cigarettes within a month of being surveyed. Smoking rates ranged from 4.3 percent in California to 9.5 percent in Kentucky.

https://www.samhsa.gov/data/sites/default/files/report_1964/ShortReport-1964.html


Conclusions

While definite conclusions cannot be drawn from this data, potential impacts can be considered:
  1. Older populations learn about the dangers of smoking after the fact of having used cigarettes.
  2. More public health innitaives have been targeted at older populations, and have subsided for younger populations.
  3. Providing information about the dangers of smoking does not change the outcome of whether a person decides to smoke.
Despite these possible conclusions, one thing can be certain:
  • Smoking is a problem for both the younger and older populations,
  • Smoking is the leading cause of death in the United States and costs the US government hundreds of billions of dollars each year, and
  • Many adult cigarette smokers actually want to quit smoking.
